03 — Going Deeper

Chunking
Strategies

Chunking is how you turn raw documents into retrievable pieces. It's one of the highest-leverage decisions in any RAG system — and one of the most underrated. Get it wrong and no amount of reranking will save you.

8 min read3 strategiesInteractive diagramCode example

Why chunking matters more than you think

Every RAG system retrieves chunks, not full documents. Before any query runs, your documents must be split into pieces, embedded, and stored. The size and shape of those pieces directly controls what context the LLM sees — and how accurate its answer is. Too large: cosine similarity dilutes and the LLM gets noise. Too small: individual chunks lack enough context to be meaningful. Overlap too low: answers spanning chunk boundaries get missed.

Rule: Chunking is always an offline step. It runs during ingestion, not at query time. Changing strategy means re-indexing your entire corpus — plan it upfront.

Strategy 01 — Fixed-Size Chunking

Split every document into chunks of exactly N tokens with M tokens of overlap between adjacent chunks. No understanding of content — just mechanical slicing. Simple, predictable, and the fastest to implement.

  • Chunk size: Typically 256–512 tokens. Smaller = more precise retrieval, less context per chunk. Larger = more context, less precise similarity scores.
  • Overlap: 10–15% of chunk size. Ensures answers spanning chunk boundaries aren't lost. Set too high and you waste index space with redundant content.
  • Best for: Homogeneous text — support tickets, emails, news articles. Anything without meaningful section structure.
Fixed-Size Chunking
Document → uniform chunks with overlap → embeddings → vector index
▶ Run — Document → Chunks with overlap → Embeddings → Vector Index
Pitfall: Fixed-size chunking cuts sentences in half. Use a sentence-aware splitter that respects sentence boundaries even within a fixed-size window.

Strategy 02 — Semantic Chunking

Instead of splitting by token count, semantic chunking groups sentences by meaning. It embeds each sentence, measures cosine similarity between adjacent sentences, and splits when similarity drops sharply — indicating a topic shift.

  • How it works: Embed sentence pairs → measure similarity → split at semantic breakpoints where similarity drops below a threshold.
  • Result: Chunks that each cover one complete topic or idea — better semantic precision at retrieval time.
  • Best for: Long-form documents with clear topic shifts — research papers, documentation, books, transcripts.
  • Tradeoff: Slower at ingestion time; chunk sizes vary widely, making it harder to predict index size.
Why it retrieves better: A query about "transformer attention" won't pull a chunk mixing attention with positional encoding — semantic chunking separated those topics. Precision improves significantly on technical content.

Strategy 03 — Recursive Chunking

Recursive chunking tries a sequence of separators in priority order: paragraph breaks → sentence breaks → word breaks → character breaks. It splits recursively using the next separator when a chunk still exceeds the target size after the current one.

  • LangChain's default uses this: RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter with separators [" ", " ", ". ", " ", ""].
  • Best for: Mixed content — code, markdown, prose in the same corpus. Handles heterogeneous documents well.
  • Practical advantage: Never cuts a paragraph mid-sentence unless absolutely necessary — preserves natural language units.
Python — RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter( chunk_size=512, chunk_overlap=64, separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " ", ""] ) chunks = splitter.split_documents(docs)
